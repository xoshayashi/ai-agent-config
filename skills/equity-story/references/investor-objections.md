# Investor Objections — Equity Story Reference

A catalogue of the objections investors raise, organised by topic. A strong
equity story does not wait for these — it **frames each one inside the story and
shows why it is acceptable.** Rehearsing the pitch exists precisely to surface
these weaknesses while they are still cheap to fix.

For each objection: the question behind it, and how to pre-empt it.

## Market size

**"Is the market actually big enough?"**
The TAM slide draws the most scrutiny of any slide. A top-down "billions" number
reads as hand-waving.
- Pre-empt: size TAM **bottom-up** — customers × price — and show the
  calculation, the sources and the segment targeted. Order it SOM → TAM so the
  logic is "take the wedge, and that wedge opens a category."

**"This is a niche; the ceiling is low."**
Common against vertical or focused plays.
- Pre-empt: redefine the market by the **job to be done** (the real competition
  is every alternative, not the category), and show the expansion path — e.g.
  vertical SaaS adding embedded fintech expands TAM 2–5×. Tie it to evidence of
  system-of-record status, not aspiration.

## Competition & defensibility

**"What stops a large incumbent from crushing you?"**
- Pre-empt: never claim "no competition" — it signals no research. Map direct
  and indirect competitors honestly. If an incumbent exists, use
  counter-positioning: show why copying you damages its own revenue, margin or
  channel. Name the durable advantage (a 7 Powers type) and the evidence for it.

**"What stops a fast follower from copying this?"**
- Pre-empt: tie the moat to something structural — network effects, switching
  costs, a cornered resource, process power — with the matching proof
  (NRR/churn for switching costs, cohort economics for network effects). "First
  mover" and "better product" are not moats.

**"Your 'data moat' — is it real?"**
The most over-claimed moat.
- Pre-empt: show accuracy gains from more data are not yet diminishing, and the
  data is proprietary and not buyable. Otherwise drop the claim.

## Unit economics & business model

**"Do the unit economics work?"**
A broken unit economic kills a buy decision regardless of the narrative.
- Pre-empt: present CAC, LTV, gross margin, payback, burn multiple and NRR as
  **verifiable actuals**, attached to the claim each one proves. The weight of
  this section rises with stage.

**"Is the high NRR hiding something?"**
- Pre-empt: disclose **gross** retention (GRR) alongside net; show expansion is
  not concentrated in a few accounts and is not just price increases; name the
  expansion path (seats / usage / modules) and its gross margin.

**"Is the revenue predictable?"**
Especially for usage-based models.
- Pre-empt: show a base commitment + usage hybrid, the predictability of
  consumption, and limited downside in a slowdown.

## Timing & execution

**"Why now — why hasn't this been built already?"**
- Pre-empt: name the inflection (technology cost, regulation, behaviour, an
  adoption threshold) and quantify it. "The market is growing" is not a
  "why now."

**"Why this team?"**
- Pre-empt: concrete, verifiable achievements and a genuine unfair advantage —
  founder-market fit — not titles or a life story.

**"Can you actually execute the plan?"**
The most common review finding in Japan: an unclear budget basis, a stretch-
target budget.
- Pre-empt: build the plan **bottom-up** with KPIs, assumptions and sensitivity;
  show budget-vs-actual accuracy over time; show progress as a line, not a dot.

## Valuation

**"Why this price?"**
- Pre-empt (private): the concrete milestones the round funds over 18–24 months
  and why the next round prices higher — risk retired × upside ahead.
- Pre-empt (IPO/public): a peer set genuinely close in growth, margin and model;
  a high multiple justified by a quantified growth premium. Never assemble a
  flattering, broken comp set — analysts see through it.

**"You're priced off your last private round."**
- Pre-empt: show pricing discipline and willingness to meet the market; a rich
  pre-IPO round creates down-round risk against a conservative IPO offer price.

## Growth vs. profitability (late-stage / IPO)

**"When do you reach the Rule of 40?"**
- Pre-empt: if not there yet, make the route across the threshold — when and how
  — the core of the story; anchor on the FCF-based Rule of 40 pre-IPO; evidence
  growth *quality* with NRR, CAC payback, magic number.

**"What happens in a downturn / recession?"**
- Pre-empt: address customer concentration, margin durability and recession
  resilience directly; do not leave the reader to raise them.

## Governance & structure (IPO)

**"Is the structure investable?"**
- Pre-empt: address float size, lock-up design, founder control and any
  conflicts of interest before the thesis is judged — a poor structure sinks the
  story regardless of its merits.

## How to use this in the story

1. Walk the topics above; pull the questions a real investor at *this stage and
   geography* would ask (cross-check with `stage-playbooks.md` and, for Japan,
   `japan-ipo.md`).
2. Fold the answers into the relevant body sections — the objection should be
   *already addressed* by the time the reader would raise it.
3. Collect the sharpest remaining questions into the explicit
   "Anticipated investor questions" section, each with a direct answer.
4. Name weaknesses together with the response strategy. Evasion is penalised
   immediately; transparency builds the credibility the whole story rests on.
