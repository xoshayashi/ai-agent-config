# Narrative Craft — Equity Story Reference

A sound thesis is necessary but not sufficient. A reader still has to *finish*
it, *remember* it, and *retell* it accurately to the partners who were not in
the room. This reference covers the craft that makes that happen.

**The governing principle:** craft carries the logic — it never fakes it. Every
technique here makes a *true* argument easier to see, feel and remember. The
moment a technique is used to make a missing argument look present, it stops
being craft and becomes hype, and a sceptical reader who notices the seam
discounts everything around it. So the test of any device below is not "is it
vivid" but "is it carrying something real."

Read this while drafting (workflow step 6) and apply it through axis B of the
self-review checklist.

## 1. Tension and stakes

Readers are pulled forward by an unresolved tension, not by a list of facts. A
well-told argument sets up a question the reader now *needs* answered, and the
need does the work of carrying them to the end. State early what is at stake —
the window that has opened, the cost of the problem, what is won or lost.

- **Do:** open each major section so it implies the next — a question raised,
  a tension not yet resolved. Make the reader feel the problem as a real cost
  before the solution appears.
- **Don't:** front-load definitions, background and company history. Don't
  resolve a tension before the reader has felt it — and don't leave one
  dangling that the story never closes.

In an equity story the spine already supplies the tension: "Why now" is the
window opening; "The problem" is the stake. Make those land as tension, not as
description.

## 2. Transportation — make the reader see it

A reader who can *picture* the situation is absorbed, argues back less, and
remembers more. Absorption comes from concrete, sensory detail and from
connecting to something the reader already knows — not from abstraction. One
specific customer in one specific Monday-morning situation transports a reader;
"a large addressable market with strong tailwinds" does not.

- **Do:** for every abstract claim, give one concrete, picturable instance.
  Anchor new ideas to something the reader already understands.
- **Don't:** run on abstractions and generalities — the reader cannot form an
  image and attention drifts. Don't let one unrealistic detail slip in; a single
  false note breaks both the immersion and the trust.

## 3. Rhetorical mechanics

A few durable devices, used sparingly:

- **Rule of three.** Three reasons, three highlights, three proof points read as
  complete and persuasive; two feel thin, five blur. Group load-bearing points
  in threes — but never pad to a third item that is not real.
- **Contrast.** Difference lands hardest in parallel structure: *"Incumbents
  sell X; we sell Y."* Use it to make differentiation sharp.
- **Abstract ↔ concrete, deliberately.** Move between the two: an abstract claim,
  then a concrete instance, then the abstract implication. A passage that stays
  fully abstract is forgettable; one that stays fully concrete has no thesis.
- **Metaphor.** One well-chosen metaphor makes a complex idea graspable. Pick
  one and hold it. Mixed or stacked metaphors collide and destroy the image.

## 4. Hook and ending

The first line decides whether the rest is read; the last line decides what is
remembered (a reader weighs an experience by its peak and its end).

- **Do:** open on tension, a sharp question, an unexpected fact, or a bold,
  defensible claim — start near the core. End on the strongest line in the
  document.
- **Don't:** open with a definition or background. Don't end on a summary or an
  administrative sign-off — a weak ending drags down the memory of the whole.
- **Pacing:** vary sentence length. Short sentences create speed and emphasis;
  longer ones invite reflection. Put a short sentence right before the point
  that must land. Uniform rhythm makes a reader disengage.

## 5. The carry-home — make it retellable

An equity story succeeds only when it is *retold* — accurately — by an investor
to partners who never met the founder. Design for that retelling. The Heath
brothers' test for ideas that stick is **SUCCES**: **S**imple (one core idea,
everything else subordinate), **U**nexpected (one non-obvious point that earns
attention), **C**oncrete (sensory, specific language), **C**redible
(verifiable detail, not adjectives), **E**motional (people engage with the
specific, not the abstract), **S**tory (a narrative the reader can repeat).

The sharpest expression of this is a **carry-home artifact**: a single phrase or
image compact enough that the reader walks out holding it and hands it on intact
("Day 1", "the system of record for X", "picks and shovels for Y"). It is not a
slogan bolted on top — it is the thesis compressed to its smallest true form.

- **Do:** decide the one core idea and subordinate everything to it. Forge one
  carry-home phrase or image and let the whole story converge on it.
- **Don't:** present several co-equal messages — the reader retains none. Don't
  ask for trust with adjectives; earn it with concrete, checkable detail.

## 6. The honesty boundary — appeal vs. hype

The danger in any persuasive document is that the craft outruns the substance.
Discipline it: a narrative gives direction, the numbers give the constraint, and
a story that contradicts its own numbers must be changed — change the story, not
the numbers. Keep the thesis in the territory of the *probable*, not merely the
*possible*; a possible story dressed as a probable one is exactly where honest
appeal becomes hype.

- **Do:** tie every device to something real — the tension to a genuine market
  window, the vivid instance to an actual customer, the urgency to a real
  constraint. Surface weaknesses early; pre-empted honesty is itself the most
  trust-building move available.
- **Don't:** use emotional appeal to cover a thin argument. Don't let rhetorical
  rhythm paper over a hole in the logic. Don't make the technique visible — a
  reader who notices the machinery reads it as manipulation.

The craft and the honesty are not in tension. Technique is a vessel for the
facts; the moment the vessel misrepresents its contents, every technique in the
document works against you.

## 7. Banned-without-evidence phrases

These words are not forbidden — they are forbidden *as a substitute for
evidence*. "Leading", "best-in-class", "world-class", "transforming",
"next-generation", "disruptive", "revolutionary", "cutting-edge",
"game-changing", "synergistic", "no competition" — each is acceptable only when
an immediately adjacent, verifiable fact earns it. "The leading X" is empty;
"the leading X — 60% of the category's GMV runs through us [fact]" is not. Used
naked, these words signal an author who has substituted a claim for proof, and a
practised reader discounts the sentence on sight.

- **Do:** replace the adjective with the fact that would justify it. If no fact
  justifies it, the claim is not yet true — say what *is* true instead.
- **Don't:** let a superlative stand alone. Don't stack them ("the leading
  next-generation platform transforming the industry") — density of hype is
  inversely correlated with substance.

## Applying this — the engagement pass

After the logical draft exists, read back over it once for craft:

1. **Hook** — does the first line create tension, or define a term?
2. **Carry-home** — is there one phrase or image the reader leaves holding?
3. **Concreteness** — does every abstract claim have one picturable instance?
4. **Contrarian core** — is the one non-obvious, against-the-consensus idea
   visible and stated plainly?
5. **Rhythm** — does sentence length vary; is the strongest line at the end?
6. **Honesty** — is every device carrying something real, and are the
   banned-without-evidence phrases all either earned or gone?

These map directly to axis B of the SKILL.md self-review checklist.
