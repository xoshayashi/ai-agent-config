# Stage Playbooks — Equity Story Reference

How the equity story changes across the company's life. The narrative spine
(Why now / Why us / Why this / Why this price) is constant; what shifts is the
**centre of gravity of the argument** and the proof the audience demands.

| Stage | Centre of gravity | Audience reads it for |
|---|---|---|
| VC seed | Team, insight, "why now" | Vision and the founder's right to win |
| VC growth | Unit economics, proof of scale | Whether the model works and compounds |
| Late-stage / pre-IPO | Predictable cash flow, Rule of 40 path | Durability and the route to public-market readiness |
| IPO | Comparables, governance, guidance | A model they can build and defend internally |
| Public IR | Delivery vs. guidance, capital allocation | Consistency and credibility over time |
| M&A exit | Synergy, accretion, integration fit | Why the company is worth more inside the buyer |

A seed argument is a short story that sells a vision; an IPO argument is a long
novel that runs on data. As the company matures, **dial down high-altitude
narrative and dial up traction, metrics, a three-statement-modellable structure,
and governance.** Putting a vision-first deck in front of an IPO book — or
burying a seed pitch in spreadsheets — fails.

## VC fundraising: seed to growth

### What the argument leans on, by stage
- **Seed:** team, the non-obvious insight, "why now," and the earliest signal.
  There is little data, so the founder's credibility and the sharpness of the
  insight carry the story.
- **Growth:** unit economics and proof the model scales — CAC, LTV, payback,
  NRR, burn multiple as verifiable actuals. The vision still matters but must
  now be underwritten by numbers.

### The equity story is also an internal document
A VC must sell the deal to their own partnership and investment committee. Write
the story so the investor can **defend it internally**: verifiable figures,
explicit logic, risks named and framed. An investment memo (Sequoia's YouTube
memo, Bessemer's Toast memo) runs: the company's essence, concisely → the size
of the market and the bet → the risks and why they are acceptable.

### Do
- Define the company in one declarative sentence — a mission, not a feature
  list. Then draw the larger arc: take the wedge, earn the right to the
  category.
- Lead with the problem; let the reader feel the customer's "Monday problem"
  before the solution appears.
- Quantify "why now" — the technology, regulatory, behavioural or cost
  inflection — and place it early.
- Show progress as **a line, not a dot**: the delta since the last raise.
- Size TAM bottom-up (customers × price), with the calculation visible.
- Time-stamp every traction figure ("0→1,000 in 8 weeks").
- Separate facts, estimates, assumptions and open uncertainties.

### Don't
- Don't fill a deck template and call it a story.
- Don't lead with the solution — that produces a product tour with no anchor.
- Don't claim "no competition" — it signals no research or no understanding.
- Don't present traction without a time axis; if traction is weak, omit the
  section rather than dress it up.
- Don't pad the team slide with biographies; use concrete, verifiable
  achievements and the genuine unfair advantage.
- Don't optimise for optics — a beautiful but thin deck reads worse than a
  plain, substantive one.

### Failure modes
Solution-first product pitches; "no competition"; beautiful-but-empty decks;
traction with no time axis; abstract metaphor vision slides; jargon and vague
"a platform for X" positioning. VCs spend on average under ~2.5 minutes on a
deck — clarity beats precision.

## Late-stage, IPO and public markets

### IPO: the equity story is the first workstream
The equity story is built early in IPO preparation and becomes the common
foundation for the prospectus/S-1 business sections, the roadshow, and analyst
education. Bankers use it to advise on public-market valuation and peer
positioning. The story propagates: research analyst → sales force → investors —
so it must stay intact when summarised at each step.

### Growth vs. profitability
Public markets want a **path to the Rule of 40** (growth rate + profit
margin ≥ 40%). If the company is not there yet, the route across the threshold —
when and how — is the core of the story.
- Pre-IPO / late-stage: anchor on the **FCF-based** Rule of 40 (adjusted EBITDA
  is scrutinised hard in public filings).
- Keep growth as the primary value driver, but evidence its *quality* with
  efficiency metrics — NRR, CAC payback, magic number.
- Do **not** lean on the old "growth first, profit later" framing. Recent
  markets reward predictable cash flow and clear revenue visibility.

### Justifying the valuation
- Use **EV/Revenue** for high-growth, low-profit companies; **P/E or
  EV/EBITDA** for stable earners.
- The multiple is set, in practice, by **peer selection.** Choose public
  comparables genuinely close in growth rate, margin and business model, then
  show where the company's distinct position sits.
- Justify a high multiple with a quantified growth premium. Backing from
  reputable investors raises the credibility of the story and the odds of a
  premium valuation.
- Do **not** assemble a flattering, broken comp set — analysts see through it
  and credibility is lost.

### What institutions and sell-side analysts want
Analysts decompose a company into **operating drivers** and model it. A story
that cannot be modelled does not persuade.
- Disclose KPI drivers (ARR, NRR, cohort retention, CAC payback, unit
  economics) so a three-statement model with scenarios/sensitivity can be built.
- Give clear growth catalysts. Set the first guidance **conservatively** to
  create a beat-and-raise starting point.
- Do not rely on an unverifiable TAM or "vision only" — a broken unit economic
  kills a buy rating regardless of the story.

### Anticipated Q&A and objection handling
Write the hard questions down in advance and **pre-empt them inside the story.**
Build a Q&A pack covering customer concentration, margin durability, competitive
pressure, recession resilience and growth visibility; rehearse it in mock
investor meetings. Name weaknesses (e.g. a temporary growth slowdown) together
with the response strategy — evasion is penalised immediately.

### Failure modes
- **Narrative detached from economics** — pricing a real-estate business as a
  tech company (WeWork); losses papered over by growth.
- **No pricing discipline** — clinging to the last private round price; one
  study finds unicorns ~48% over fair value on average. Show willingness to
  meet the market.
- **Aggressive first guidance** — a strong first guide invites a
  miss-and-lower; conservative guidance creates the beat-and-raise base.
- **Supply/governance neglect** — thin float, poor lock-up design, founder
  control or conflicts of interest sink the story before the thesis is heard.

## M&A exit / strategic-buyer narrative

When the audience is a **strategic acquirer** rather than a financial investor,
the equity story changes shape. A financial investor underwrites a standalone
return (IRR, the path to a future exit); a strategic buyer underwrites
**synergy** — what the target is worth *inside the acquirer*.

- The centre of gravity moves from "why this company compounds on its own" to
  **"why this company is worth more to you than to anyone else."** Lead with the
  synergy thesis: revenue synergies (cross-sell, channel, geography), cost
  synergies, and capability/IP/talent the buyer cannot build fast enough.
- Justify the price as **accretion**, not standalone valuation — the deal's
  effect on the buyer's EBITDA, margin, growth rate or strategic position.
  Comparable *transactions* (precedent M&A multiples) matter more than
  comparable public companies.
- Pre-empt integration risk explicitly: technical fit, culture, customer
  overlap, key-person retention. A strategic buyer's biggest fear is a deal that
  destroys value in integration.
- Keep this distinct from the IPO story. If the user has not specified the
  exit path, ask before defaulting; if they want both an IPO and an M&A framing,
  produce the standalone thesis once and append a strategic-buyer synergy layer.

For the financial mechanics of an M&A exit (deal value, accretion/dilution,
returns), use `startup-financial-modeling`; this skill owns the synergy
narrative.
