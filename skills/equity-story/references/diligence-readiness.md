# Diligence Readiness

The equity story wins the meeting. It does not win the deal. Between the second
meeting and the wired money sits the **investment-evaluation phase** — due
diligence, the IC (investment committee) memo, the partnership vote, and term
negotiation — and it runs for weeks or months. A story can be entirely
persuasive in the room and still die there — by some practitioner accounts,
diligence-stage findings account for a large share of deals that fail after a
strong first impression.

Read this when the work has to clear that phase, not just earn a follow-up:
late-stage private rounds, IPO, growth rounds, any raise where a real diligence
team will verify the claims. For an early-stage story it still applies, at the
depth that stage warrants — see *Depth scales with stage* below.

## The shift: from one reader to a process

In the meeting the story is *read*. In diligence it is *checked*. The model is
rebuilt from source data; metrics are recomputed; customers are called; the cap
table, the contracts, the IP, the code are audited; the partnership's sharpest
sceptic is paid to find the reason not to do the deal.

The practitioner shorthand is blunt: **the pitch is the teaser; the data room is
the verification.** The two are sequential, not the same artifact — a story does
not need to *contain* the evidence, but every claim in it must be *consistent
with* evidence that exists and can be produced. One principle carries that:

**The equity story is the front door to the data room.** Every load-bearing
claim points — explicitly or by clear implication — to the artifact that proves
it. A claim with no verification path is not diligence-ready, however well it
reads. Everything below is a corollary of that one frame.

The skill's evidence legend (`[fact]` / `[derived]` / `[estimate]` /
`[assumption]` / `[open]`) records the *author's confidence*. Diligence readiness
adds the next question: **how would a diligence team verify this, and where?**
A `[fact]` with no verification path is an assertion a sceptic cannot check —
and what a diligence team cannot check, it discounts.

## Depth scales with stage

Diligence depth is not uniform — match it to the stage, the same way market
sizing does. Do not pad, and do not let an early-stage story pretend to a data
room it cannot have.

- **Seed** — light diligence, typically the shortest, centred on team, market
  and the early signal of product-market fit. Founder reference and background
  checks, design-partner or early-customer calls, basic IP hygiene (is the IP
  cleanly assigned), a sane cap table. The data room is thin, and that is
  correct. The story should still name what little evidence exists and where it
  sits.
- **Growth** — rigorous financial and commercial diligence over four to eight
  weeks: the model rebuilt, cohorts decomposed, unit economics recomputed from
  billing data, customer references called against the retention claims.
- **Late-stage / IPO** — audited financials, a quality-of-earnings analysis,
  full legal and regulatory review, comparable-company and governance scrutiny;
  for an IPO, the underwriter's review is the most exacting of all.

A diligence claim a stage cannot support is worse than a missing one — it is the
stage-fit failure the skill already warns against, surfaced under audit.

## The four diligence workstreams

A diligence team divides the company along four lines. A diligence-ready story
anticipates what each will examine and hands it a starting point.

- **Financial** — the model rebuilt from source; revenue, margin, burn and
  runway recomputed. The core lens is **quality of earnings**: not the headline
  number but whether it is *recurring, repeatable and clean* — one-time items
  stripped out, accounting distortions normalised, revenue recognition checked
  against the contracts. Metric *definitions* are pinned down (how ARR is
  measured, the NRR formula, what loads into CAC), and growth is reconciled with
  a bridge (opening balance + new + expansion − contraction − churn = closing
  balance). Plus the cap table and prior-round terms.
- **Commercial** — customer reference calls, pipeline quality, win/loss and
  churn reasons, revenue concentration, the durability of the moat in practice.
- **Technical / product** — does the product do what the story says it does;
  what is shipped versus on the roadmap; architecture, scalability, security
  posture, technical debt.
- **Legal / IP** — IP ownership and assignment, freedom to operate, material
  contracts, litigation, regulatory standing, corporate and equity records.

## Turn every load-bearing claim into a checkable one

For each load-bearing claim — the figures and assertions that, if false, break
the thesis — do three things.

1. **Point to the system of record.** Name, or clearly imply, the artifact that
   substantiates it: the billing-system export, the signed contract, the
   clinical-trial registry ID, the patent number, the primary-source
   publication, the audited statement. The story need not reproduce the
   artifact; it must make clear one exists and what it is.
2. **Convert adjective-facts to number-facts — with the definition stated.**
   "Usage is rising" is not checkable; "weekly active accounts grew from 8 to 22
   of 30 design partners between January and April" is. State the definition,
   the basis and the exclusions — because a diligence team will otherwise impose
   its own and recompute the number adversarially from raw source data. A metric
   has more than one defensible definition; founder-reported retention routinely
   falls on recomputation when one-time revenue was blended into recurring or
   mid-term downgrades were not netted out. State which definition you used, and
   make sure the same number is identical everywhere it appears — a metric that
   disagrees with itself across two pages discredits the whole document, even
   when the cause is an honest slip. When a number needs normalising — for
   seasonality, or because a blended average spans an uneven base — show the
   normalised or decomposed figure itself; telling the reader to adjust it
   mentally leaves the load-bearing number unstated.
3. **Name the load-bearing entities.** An anonymized target, customer, patent or
   institution cannot be verified — diligence cannot even begin. A real document
   names them. Where a name is genuinely withheld for confidentiality, mark it
   `[open]` with the reason and the stage at which it will be disclosed, so the
   reader sees a deliberate hold, not a gap.

## The data-room index

The strongest equity stories carry, or imply, a map: each load-bearing claim to
the artifact that backs it. A diligence team works from a small set of standard
categories — corporate and legal, cap table and securities, financials,
commercial and customer, product and technology, IP, and people — and a
well-run raise gives them a hyperlinked index across those categories rather
than a folder dump. Even before the data room is built, listing what each claim
*will* be substantiated by does two things — it signals diligence readiness to
the reader, and it tells the company exactly what to assemble. A short "evidence
behind this story" appendix, or a verification pointer beside each major claim,
is enough; it need not be elaborate.

## Disclose the landmines before diligence finds them

A damaging fact discovered by diligence can kill a deal. The *same fact*,
disclosed in the story, usually does not — disclosure converts a surprise into a
known, priced risk, and it keeps the company in control of the narrative.
Practitioners are blunt about the order of operations: the most experienced
operators run diligence on themselves well ahead of a raise specifically to find
and fix these before an investor does.

This is distinct from preempting investor objections: objections attack the
*thesis* ("why won't this work"); landmines are *hygiene and structural facts*
that surface under audit. Walk these categories — not an exhaustive list — and
put the ones that bite for this company on the table:

- **IP ownership** — assignment from prior employers, contractors and founders,
  and any trade-secret or non-compete exposure carried from prior employment;
  university or institutional license terms and reach-through royalties;
  freedom-to-operate exposure; open-source license propagation. A missing IP
  assignment is, by practitioner consensus, the single most common deal-killer
  in diligence — name the IP position explicitly rather than leaving it silent.
- **Cap table** — SAFE or convertible-note overhang, option-pool size, advisor
  equity, prior secondary sales, unusual or off-market prior-round terms,
  departed co-founders holding large stakes.
- **Cash** — current balance, burn rate, months of runway, any prior bridge.
- **Customer concentration** — the share of revenue in the top handful of
  accounts, and contract renewal exposure.
- **Revenue structure** — seasonality, one-time versus recurring mix, prepaid
  versus in-period recognition, the measurement date.
- **Security & compliance** — SOC 2, data handling, regulatory posture —
  especially where sensitive data is involved.
- **Key person** — founder dependency, retention, visa or relocation risk,
  single points of failure in the team.
- **History** — prior failed fundraising or partnering talks, pivots, past
  litigation or disputes.

Disclosure is not confession. State the fact, then the mitigation or why it is
acceptable — the same move the story already uses for its weaknesses.

## The story must feed the IC memo

After the meeting, the deal partner does not just decide — they must *write up*
the investment as an internal memo and defend it to the partnership, often
against a sceptic whose explicit job is to argue the other side. That memo, not
the pitch, is "the document argued over when the founder is not in the room."
The equity story's real second job is to be liftable into it with minimal
rewriting.

IC memos converge on a standard shape: an executive summary with the thesis and
the recommendation, then market, team, product and traction, business model and
unit economics, and risks with mitigations. The equity-story output format in
`SKILL.md` already maps onto this — write each section so the deal partner can
carry it across, not paraphrase it. The practitioner test: **could a partner who
never met the founder defend the five core points — market, traction, unit
economics, team, risk — in ninety seconds from this document alone?**

Two things make a memo defensible, and the equity story should already contain
them:

- **A quantified downside, not just a qualitative one.** The story's reversal
  triggers say *what observation* would break the thesis. The IC memo also needs
  *how much is at stake*: a short **bear case** — the loss scenario named and
  roughly sized (the revenue plateau and the multiple it earns, the clinical
  failure and the residual platform value, the down round and the recovery
  path). Strong memos carry three scenarios — base, bull and **bear** — and do
  not hide the bear. Size the loss that actually reaches the investor: for
  growth-stage rounds and later, account not for enterprise value alone but for
  *their* outcome after the preference waterfall — liquidation preferences, SAFE
  or note conversion, anti-dilution — which can make the downside materially
  worse than a headline EV mark-down suggests. A
  thesis that has visibly costed its own failure is more credible, not less.
  Carry one honest paragraph a partner can lift; defer the full model to
  `startup-financial-modeling` — the story carries the *narrative* of the
  downside, not the spreadsheet.
- **Each major risk paired with a leading indicator and a concrete mitigation.**
  A mitigation that is an aspiration ("we will hire a great VP of Sales") is
  read as a diligence that was itself an aspiration. Pair each top risk with the
  *observable early-warning metric* that says it is materialising — which is
  also the de facto kill criterion — and a mitigation grounded in something
  real. A useful way to generate this set is a **pre-mortem**: assume the
  investment has failed, ask what went wrong, and work back to the indicators
  that would have shown it early.

## The test

Diligence readiness is a second gate, applied after the meeting gate:

> Could a diligence team verify the load-bearing claims from the paths the story
> gives, find no deal-killing surprise it should have disclosed, and let a
> partner write a defensible IC memo — with a quantified downside — that holds
> up against the partnership's sceptic?

Until that holds at the depth the stage warrants, the story may win the room
but will not survive the process that follows it.
