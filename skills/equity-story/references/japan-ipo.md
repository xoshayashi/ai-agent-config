# Japan IPO & Fundraising — Equity Story Reference

Japanese IPO and fundraising have their own conventions. Read this when the
context is a Japanese IPO, a Japanese pre-IPO round, or IR for a TSE-listed
company. It supplements — does not replace — `stage-playbooks.md`.

## 1. What the equity story means in Japan, and the underwriter review

In Japan the equity story is both an investor-facing narrative (growth strategy,
competitive advantage, market opportunity) **and** the logical case that proves
the business plan's *feasibility* (蓋然性) — the case that has to pass the lead
underwriter's review (引受審査) and the exchange review. The core is the union of
**a compelling narrative and demonstrable feasibility.**

- **Do.** Build a rough scenario from management interviews, then reinforce and
  revise it repeatedly against market data and budget-vs-actual results. Have
  the CFO lead both the governance and the growth narrative.
- **Don't.** Use unsupported superlatives or inflated market sizes — the
  underwriter review (typically 5–6 rounds of written questions, 100–200 items
  each) will break them. Don't turn a top-down "stretch target" into the
  business plan; that is the most common review finding.

## 2. 成長可能性資料 — the TSE Growth growth-potential disclosure

> Note: the **TSE Growth Market** is the venue renamed from "Mothers" in the
> April 2022 TSE market restructuring. Older sources may still call it Mothers —
> it is the same venue.

Companies listing on the **TSE Growth Market** must disclose "事業計画及び成長
可能性に関する事項" — material on the business plan and growth potential. Its
purpose is to give investors a reasonable basis for judgement on companies that
are "relatively higher-risk in terms of operating track record." It is the
equity story rendered into an official disclosure document.

**Structure (items required; order and merging are free, so favour narrative
flow):**
1. Business model — what the business does, the revenue structure.
2. Market environment — market size, competition.
3. Sources of competitiveness — management resources, advantages.
4. Business plan — growth strategy, management KPIs, the profit plan with its
   assumptions, and progress.
5. Risk information — recognised risks and countermeasures.

- **Do.** Disclose first on the listing day, then update at least once per
  fiscal year, and promptly when the plan is revised. Publish via TDnet, PDF,
  ≤10 MB.
- **Don't.** Leave the profit-plan assumptions vague — continuous disclosure
  means progress is tracked, and a miss must be explained at the next update.

## 3. Prospectus drafting: 事業の内容 / 事業等のリスク / 対処すべき課題

The top priority is **consistency** — the prospectus, the 成長可能性資料 and the
roadshow material must not disagree on figures, market size, competitors or
KPIs. The FSA publishes good-practice collections encouraging clear disclosure.

- **事業の内容 (business description).** Avoid jargon; explain the revenue
  structure and which businesses drive profit, in plain language.
- **事業等のリスク (risks).** For each risk, state the likelihood, timing,
  impact and countermeasure as a set, alongside management's materiality
  judgement. A risk map helps order them.
- **対処すべき課題 (issues to address).** Tie issues to the management policy,
  with concrete actions and achievement KPIs — not an abstract statement of
  resolve.

## 4. 想定問答集 — the anticipated-Q&A pack

The 想定問答集 secures the quality of management's answers in both the listing-
review hearings and the roadshow. The lead underwriter accumulates patterns of
past questions; prepare it jointly and comprehensively.

- **Do.** Prepare answers for the angles different institutional investors take;
  rehearse until management can answer flexibly from a deep understanding of the
  company. Run a SWOT analysis — questions on weaknesses are frequent in review.
- **Don't.** Evade questions on weaknesses, litigation or dependency risks.
  Don't disclose, verbally, information beyond the prospectus (avoid information
  asymmetry).

## 5. The Growth → Prime market path

Frame the equity story from listing day around the scale-up path implied by the
**continued-listing standards and the route to Prime.** From March 2030, the
TSE Growth market-cap standard 10 years after listing rises from ¥4bn to ¥10bn
and the deadline shortens from 10 years to 5; moving up to Prime requires market
cap of ¥25bn or more.

- **Do.** Make the mid-term profit plan and growth strategy explainable as the
  path "market cap above ¥10bn → ¥25bn." Weave a staged upgrade of governance
  and disclosure toward Prime-level into the story.
- **Don't.** Tell a short-term story that treats the listing itself as the goal.

## 6. Comparable-company valuation (PER / PSR) in Japan

The offer price is set mainly by the comparable-listed-company method (the
market approach). Profitable companies are valued on **PER**; loss-making,
high-growth SaaS/IT/biotech on **PSR** — this is Japanese market practice.

- **Do.** Select primarily *domestic* peers genuinely close in business
  substance, growth rate and profit structure; use overseas comparables only as
  support. Rough guides: PER median ~20–30×, PSR median ~4–5× (PSR is often
  quoted on next-year revenue). These bands move materially with market
  conditions — verify them against current JPX/peer data before citing them in
  a live document.
- **Don't.** Justify a rich price by lining up only high-multiple overseas
  names. Japanese IPO opening-price formation is conservative and you will be
  asked to account for it. Don't leave the peer-selection rationale vague —
  align it with the management KPIs in the 成長可能性資料.

## 7. Weaknesses the review and investors probe — and how to get ahead

The most common weakness is the **feasibility of the business plan.** Typical
findings: "the basis for the budget is unclear," "the budget is a stretch
target." A downward revision in the period just before listing is read as proof
of low planning quality and is close to fatal.

- **Do.** Build the business plan bottom-up; show KPIs, assumptions and
  sensitivity; demonstrate budget-vs-actual accuracy from well before listing.
  Disclose customer/business concentration, regulatory and key-person
  dependencies yourself, with countermeasures.
- **Don't.** Aim for a landing that is neither too conservative nor too
  optimistic in the pre-listing period — leave no downward-revision risk. Don't
  hide weaknesses; build the response to the SWOT "weaknesses" into the story.

## 8. Japanese VC fundraising (incl. pre-IPO) vs. the US

Japanese startup exits are IPO-heavy (the US has a much deeper M&A market).
Japanese VC fund capital comes roughly one-third each from operating companies
and banks/insurers — versus the US, where ~60% comes from pension funds — so
Japanese capital is more conservative and shorter-horizon, and round sizes are
far smaller.

- **Do.** In Japan, build a story with the **IPO as the explicit exit** and a
  feasible growth path to listing. For a CVC counterpart, also speak to business
  synergy. Weight the story toward demonstrated achievement of near-term KPIs
  and a clear route to monetisation — Japanese investors prize feasibility over
  a giant TAM × exponential-growth narrative.
- **Don't.** Import a US-style large, long, loss-leading story wholesale — it
  does not fit Japan's capital supply structure or exit norms. Don't inflate the
  pre-IPO round valuation; it creates down-round risk against a conservative
  IPO offer price (comparable-company method, conservative opening price).
