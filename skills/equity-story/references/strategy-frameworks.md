# Strategy Frameworks — Equity Story Reference

A dispatchable catalogue of the strategy frameworks an equity story draws on to
argue **why a company keeps winning** and **how it creates value**. Each entry
is summarised as: **essence / when to use / proof investors expect / pitfalls**.

Use the dispatch table first to find the right framework, then read its entry.

**Cross-cutting principle:** opportunity frameworks (blue ocean, category
design, jobs-to-be-done) describe *how big the prize is* — they do **not**
prove durability. Always pair them with a moat framework (7 Powers, network
effects) so the story argues the size of the prize *and* the reason the company
keeps it. Investors look for both **Benefit** and **Barrier**, and for a
quantified proof point behind every claim.

## Dispatch table

| Situation in the equity story | Reach for |
|---|---|
| Entering a market with a large incumbent | Counter-Positioning; Unbundling |
| Why the company gets stronger as it scales | Network Effects; Flywheel; Scale Economies |
| New / unmeasured market; justifying a premium valuation | Category Design; Blue Ocean |
| Go-to-market order; who the first customer is | Crossing the Chasm / Beachhead; Jobs-to-be-Done |
| Why an intermediary captures the value, not suppliers | Aggregation Theory |
| Rebutting "the niche TAM is too small" | Vertical SaaS + embedded fintech; Jobs-to-be-Done |
| Growth is certain but the winner is not | Picks and Shovels |
| The quantitative backbone: capital efficiency, compounding | Land & Expand / NRR; PLG; Usage-Based Pricing |
| The skeleton of the whole moat section | 7 Powers (tie each line to ≥1 Power) |
| Durability of an AI / data product | Data Network Effects (mind diminishing returns) |
| Drug development; value turns on clinical milestones, not revenue | Biotech / Pharma clinical-pipeline thesis |
| Hardware / deep tech; the path to manufacturing economics | Hardware / Deep Tech cost-curve thesis |
| Fintech; which revenue structure sets the valuation frame | Fintech: take-rate vs. balance-sheet |

## A. Moat & competitive-advantage theory

### 1. 7 Powers
**Essence.** Durable advantage reduces to seven types, each requiring *both* a
Benefit (cost advantage or price premium) and a Barrier (something that stops
imitation): Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching
Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, Process Power.
**When to use.** The backbone of the moat section. Tie every business line to at
least one named Power so "why incumbents and fast followers cannot take this" is
structural, not asserted.
**Proof investors expect.** The quantitative signal matching the Power — Scale:
the unit-cost gap vs. competitors; Switching Costs: NRR, churn, the real cost of
migration; Branding: price premium, share of unbranded search; Cornered
Resource: exclusive contracts, patents, key-person retention; Process Power: a
productivity gap that widens over years.
**Pitfalls.** Mistaking "a strong product" for a Power (a Power *must* have a
Barrier). Listing several Powers shallowly instead of proving one deeply.

### 2. Counter-Positioning
**Essence.** Adopt a business model the incumbent cannot *rationally* copy
because doing so would damage its own existing business (Netflix vs.
Blockbuster).
**When to use.** Entering a market with a strong incumbent — the answer to "why
won't the giant just crush you?"
**Proof investors expect.** The incumbent's revenue/margin structure, and a
concrete account of how copying you destroys its existing revenue, margin,
channel, or share price. Observed evidence that the incumbent is hesitating.
**Pitfalls.** Underestimating an incumbent that follows a few years late.
Confusing "just cheaper" with counter-positioning. A new model with no moat of
its own once copied.

### 3. Network Effects
**Essence.** Each new user raises the value for other users — a positive
feedback loop. Types (NfX's 16): direct (physical, protocol, personal),
two-sided / marketplace, data, tech-performance, social (language, tribal,
bandwagon). Direct effects are strongest.
**When to use.** Platforms, marketplaces, social, data-accumulating products —
"why the leader gets stronger as it grows."
**Proof investors expect.** Cohort engagement improving over time; superior unit
economics in dense geographies/categories; evidence of approaching critical
mass / liquidity.
**Pitfalls.** Passing off raw "user count" as a network effect. Confusing a
local network (e.g. ride-hail) with a global one. Ignoring multi-homing and
disintermediation.

### 4. Data Moats / Data Network Effects
**Essence.** Accumulated usage data improves the product, which drives more
usage. The most over-claimed moat — treat with discipline.
**When to use.** AI/ML products, recommendation, credit, optimisation engines.
**Proof investors expect.** Evidence that accuracy gains from more data are
*not* yet diminishing; the uniqueness of the data (proprietary, not buyable);
measured data → product → acquisition loop.
**Pitfalls.** Diminishing returns arriving early. Commodity data that does not
differentiate. Confusing "having data" with "winning on data."

### 5. Aggregation Theory
**Essence.** The internet modularises supply; with zero distribution and
transaction cost, whoever owns the best user experience owns demand, and
demand-side scale economies tip the market. Three conditions: modular supply,
direct customer relationship, zero marginal supply cost.
**When to use.** Intermediary / aggregator businesses — why *you* capture the
value rather than the suppliers.
**Proof investors expect.** Indicators that you own demand (direct traffic,
app-first); evidence supply is commoditised and substitutable; a measured UX
advantage.
**Pitfalls.** Supply that does not modularise and instead holds bargaining power
(premium content owners). Regulatory targeting of aggregators.

## B. Market & positioning archetypes

### 6. Category Design / Category King
**Essence.** Rather than compete inside an existing category, *design a new
one*, fix a point of view (POV) about the problem in customers' minds, and seize
it with a "lightning strike." Per Play Bigger's research, the category king
captures roughly three-quarters (~76%) of the category's market value.
**When to use.** New-market-creation stories; markets that cannot yet be
measured; justifying a premium valuation.
**Proof investors expect.** A clear POV (the problem defined, a future painted);
your dominance in category recall; analyst/media recognition of the category;
share of leads/deals originating from the category name.
**Pitfalls.** Confusing category creation with marketing hype. The market does
not actually exist (no customer pain). Education cost burns the runway.

### 7. Blue Ocean Strategy
**Essence.** Pursue differentiation and low cost *simultaneously* — "value
innovation." Use ERRC (Eliminate / Reduce / Raise / Create) to reconstruct the
industry's value factors and create uncontested space.
**When to use.** Explaining a position that escapes a red ocean; arguing cost
structure and differentiation together.
**Proof investors expect.** A strategy canvas showing divergence from
competitors; real cost savings from Eliminate/Reduce; non-customers won over.
**Pitfalls.** A blue ocean with no imitation barrier turns red fast — pair it
with 7 Powers for durability. Confusing "a niche" with "a blue ocean."

### 8. Crossing the Chasm / Beachhead
**Essence.** Between early adopters (who buy a vision) and the early majority
(pragmatists who buy proof) lies a chasm. Cross it by *completely* dominating
one narrow segment (the beachhead) and then expanding into adjacent segments
like bowling pins. A "whole product" — the complete, integrated solution — is
required.
**When to use.** The go-to-market section — "who do we win first, and how does
it spread?" Ordering TAM/SAM/SOM.
**Proof investors expect.** Dominant share and referenceable customers in the
chosen segment; whole-product completeness; proof of expansion into the next
segment (the second pin).
**Pitfalls.** A beachhead too narrow or too broad. Reusing visionary messaging
on pragmatists (references do not transfer across segments).

### 9. Jobs-to-be-Done
**Essence.** Customers "hire" a product to get a specific job done; jobs have
functional, emotional and social dimensions. The competition is every
alternative way of getting the job done, not just the category (a milkshake
competes with a banana, and with boredom).
**When to use.** Redefining TAM; identifying the real source of demand and the
real competitors; the logic for product expansion.
**Proof investors expect.** A job-based TAM; data on "what customers switched
from"; correlation between job satisfaction and retention/expansion.
**Pitfalls.** Defining the job conveniently after the fact. Seeing only the
functional job and missing the emotional/social. A job too small to be a
business.

## C. Business-model & growth archetypes

### 10. Platform / Marketplace Flywheel
**Essence.** Supply and demand call each other in a self-reinforcing loop
(the Amazon flywheel) — the business-model implementation of network effects.
**When to use.** Explaining the growth engine of a two-sided market; the future
path to lower CAC.
**Proof investors expect.** Liquidity metrics (match rate, fill rate); two-sided
cohort retention; rising organic share; a durable take rate.
**Pitfalls.** An unsolved cold-start problem; a chronically short side;
multi-homing and leakage (transactions off-platform).

### 11. Vertical SaaS
**Essence.** Own the deep workflow of one industry to become its "operating
system / system of record," then expand TAM 2–5× with embedded fintech
(payments, lending).
**When to use.** Rebutting the "niche TAM is too small" objection; the story of
a revenue mix evolving from SaaS toward fintech.
**Proof investors expect.** Evidence of system-of-record status (high NRR, low
churn); fintech attach rate and gross margin; an industry-specific data asset.
**Pitfalls.** A SaaS-only TAM that genuinely is small. Fintech revenue carries
lower multiples and credit risk — it cannot be valued without proof of the mix
shift. Underestimating how hard horizontal expansion is.

### 12. Picks and Shovels
**Essence.** In a gold rush, sell the shovels (certain) rather than mine
(uncertain). Win the infrastructure/tooling layer every player needs without
betting on which end-application wins.
**When to use.** A new market where growth is certain but the winner is not
(AI, fintech, Web3).
**Proof investors expect.** Diversification across multiple end-markets and
customers; revenue tracking overall market growth; switching costs on demand.
**Pitfalls.** The infrastructure layer itself commoditising into price
competition. Sharing the downside if the end-market fails to take off. A few
large customers building in-house.

### 13. Land & Expand / Net Revenue Retention
**Essence.** Land small, then grow account value through usage, seats and
upsell. NRR above 100% means the business grows even with zero new logos — a
bucket that does not leak.
**When to use.** The quantitative backbone of capital efficiency and compounding
growth.
**Proof investors expect.** NRR (best-in-class 120–125%; above 120% is
exceptional); net expansion by cohort; the named expansion path (seats / usage /
modules); the gross margin of the expansion.
**Pitfalls.** High NRR masking high gross churn; expansion concentrated in a few
large accounts; NRR inflated by price increases; a hidden weak GRR.

### 14. Product-Led Growth (PLG)
**Essence.** The product itself is the acquisition, conversion and expansion
engine; freemium/trials let users discover value themselves. Per OpenView's PLG
benchmarks, PLG companies grow roughly 2× faster, and product-qualified leads
convert 5–6× better than MQLs.
**When to use.** Explaining low CAC, fast growth, efficient GTM; the bottom-up
adoption story.
**Proof investors expect.** Free→paid conversion (PQL→paid 20–30%);
time-to-value; organic/viral share of acquisition; self-serve unit economics.
**Pitfalls.** No sales motion for the enterprise transition; free users as a
cost drag; freemium that never converts; ACV too small.

### 15. Usage-Based Pricing
**Essence.** Charge in proportion to the value the customer realises; more usage
expands revenue automatically without an upsell negotiation. ~78% of
infrastructure/API companies used a consumption component by 2025.
**When to use.** Arguing value alignment and a natural lift in NRR; the revenue
model for AI/infrastructure businesses.
**Proof investors expect.** A hybrid of base commitment + usage; predictability
of consumption; correlation of usage with NRR; limited downside.
**Pitfalls.** Hard-to-forecast, recession-sensitive revenue; customer incentive
to optimise spend down; instability of pure usage with no commitment.

### 16. Bundling / Unbundling
**Essence.** Bundle features to raise value, defensibility and price; or
unbundle — a focused single feature peels off part of an incumbent's suite.
"All profit comes from bundling and unbundling" (Jim Barksdale).
**When to use.** Unbundling as the wedge for market entry; bundling as the logic
for expansion, moat-strengthening and higher LTV.
**Proof investors expect.** Unbundling side: a clear 10× advantage on the single
feature plus a wedge→expansion path. Bundling side: attach rate, cross-sell,
lower churn from the bundle.
**Pitfalls.** An unbundler stuck as "a feature, not a business." A bundler
inferior to specialists on each feature. Ignoring re-bundling counterattacks.

## D. Industry-specific proof anchors

These are not moats — they are *proof anchors*. A SaaS-shaped equity story rests
on NRR, payback and a flywheel; a drug, a hardware company or a lender does not.
Each entry below names what the load-bearing evidence actually is for that
industry. Use it to decide *what proof the story must carry*, then still draw
the durable advantage from a section A moat framework.

### 17. Biotech / Pharma — the clinical-pipeline thesis
**Essence.** The thesis values an *un-approved* clinical pipeline as
risk-adjusted future value. The standard tool is **rNPV** — each phase's
probability of success (PoS) multiplied into future cash flows, then discounted.
Investors fund **de-risking** — what the *next* phase transition will prove —
not the end state. Two narrative axes: single-asset (reaches clinical data fast,
market-resilient) vs. platform (sells the *repeatability* of one engine across
many assets); and the mechanism of action plus the regulatory path (orphan,
breakthrough, accelerated approval).
**When to use.** Drug-development companies, and any thesis where value turns on
clinical milestones and regulatory exclusivity rather than on revenue.
**Proof investors expect.** Clinical data (effect size, safety, statistical
significance vs. control); phase-specific PoS, read against base rates (roughly
10% overall from Phase 1 to approval — lower in oncology, materially higher in
rare disease); regulator interactions (end-of-phase meeting minutes, agreed
endpoints, Fast Track / Breakthrough designations); IP runway and the overlap of
patent and regulatory exclusivity; KOL (key-opinion-leader) support; an explicit
rNPV build.
**Pitfalls.** Single-asset dependence (one data readout can erase the company);
optimistic PoS that ignores the base rate for the indication; a TAM inflated to
the whole prevalent population with no diagnosis / access / share haircut; an
empty "platform" claim with no asset yet in the clinic. Investors want a
**clinical line of sight** — an asset past the early, highest-risk gate.

### 18. Hardware / Deep Tech — the path to manufacturing economics
**Essence.** The thesis is not "is the technology better" — it is the *economic
path to manufacturing it at scale*. Backbone: a durable technical edge; the
cost-curve from **FOAK** (first-of-a-kind, high cost) to **NOAK** (Nth-of-a-kind,
low cost); the gross-margin ramp; and capex with a path to mature unit
economics. The central concept is the **learning curve** (Wright's Law) — unit
cost falls a roughly fixed percentage with each doubling of cumulative output.
**When to use.** Hardware, deep tech, climate, and other manufacturing-heavy
businesses.
**Proof investors expect.** Technical-validation milestones (technology
readiness level, third-party replication, bench → pilot → commercial data);
**yield** (good-unit rate) and a credible plan to improve it; the cost curve —
unit cost today vs. at the next round, with the learning-rate assumption stated;
backlog and LOIs (evidence a buyer exists, not just that the tech works);
secured manufacturing partners and supply chain; certifications.
**Pitfalls.** Expecting software multiples on a capex-heavy, slow-margin
business; underestimating capex (hidden install / logistics / commissioning
costs run well above the headline) and the difficulty of scale; conflating
technical risk with market risk — both must be retired separately; overclaiming
"deep tech" for what is a routine recombination of known parts.

### 19. Fintech — take-rate vs. balance-sheet
**Essence.** Fintech is not one business model, and the first move is to state
**which revenue structure** it is — because that sets the valuation frame.
*Take-rate / fee revenue* (payments, embedded finance, BaaS) is capital-light,
scales like software, and earns a higher multiple. *Balance-sheet / spread
revenue* (lending, parts of neobanking, full-stack insurance) funds assets off
its own balance sheet, carries credit risk and regulatory capital, and is valued
on risk-adjusted spread or book value — *not* on revenue multiples. The thesis
states the current mix and shows an evidenced path toward the higher-multiple
structure. The moat is "**fin, not tech**" — regulatory licences, risk-bearing
capacity, transaction data and funding-cost advantage, not UI; a licence is both
a moat and a constraint, so frame the compliance apparatus as evidence of
operating maturity and quantify the capital drag on returns.
**When to use.** Payments, lending, neobanks, embedded finance, insurtech.
**Proof investors expect.** The volume → revenue bridge stated explicitly
(transaction volume × take-rate = revenue — TPV is *not* revenue); risk-adjusted
unit economics; for any lending or insurance balance-sheet exposure — the
credit-loss rate, cohort / vintage delinquency, funding cost, regulatory-capital
adequacy, and behaviour modelled through a full credit cycle; a path to
profitability.
**Pitfalls.** Presenting GMV / TPV (or gross written premium) as if it were
revenue; optimistic credit-loss assumptions, or low loss rates from immature
vintages shown as steady-state; a credit model never tested through a downturn;
applying SaaS multiples to spread revenue; treating a licence only as a moat
while ignoring its capital and compliance cost.
