# Content And Story

## Causal composition

Choose the exhibit from the relationship the audience must understand: change, cause and effect, comparison, hierarchy, sequence, or decision. Establish one reading direction and one dominant transformation, tension, or evidence object. Use scale, alignment, and proximity to make supporting elements subordinate. Integrate the takeaway into the reading path or its immediate caption so the conclusion follows from the exhibit.

Review the composition abstractly across topics. The exhibit passes when a viewer can identify where to start, what changes or contrasts, and what implication follows without relying on decorative symmetry. Repair by clarifying direction, strengthening the dominant object, merging a detached conclusion into the exhibit, reducing radial label placement and long vertical text, and redistributing the occupied regions to match the intended center of gravity.

Body improvements retain the approved deck master. Compare every revised slide with the pilot for H1/subtitle geometry, left anchor, canvas tone, palette roles, outer shell, and footer mode; preserve the improved logic while restoring any drift in the shared master.

## Single-message canvas review

Review header, upper body, lower body, and footer as one continuous canvas. Each occupied region supports the same governing thought and joins the selected reading path. Approve one H1/subtitle identity and one connected body silhouette. Treat a duplicated header, a second independently framed exhibit, a split-slide composition, or content copied from a reference page as a blocker rather than a density or styling issue.

Use this file for brief-to-deck reasoning.

## Deck Contract

Resolve purpose, audience, required decision, intended use, language, source strictness, and palette. Use embedded ACT defaults when the brief supports them; clarify decisions that materially change the result.

## Story Logic

1. State one governing thought.
2. Map setup, tension, proof turn, and landing.
3. Write action titles whose read-through forms one argument.
4. Give every slide one primary focal object and one dominant structure.
5. Match every title to a named exhibit element that proves it.
6. Use MECE support and declare inductive or deductive body logic.

## Ordinal Semantics

Apply `ordinal_semantics_lock` before adding `01/02/03`, `Step N`, circled numbers, or numbered badges. Use ordinals when order itself carries meaning: a timeline, phase roadmap, staged flow, ranked list, deductive premise chain, swimlane, or causal staircase. Use parallel position, alignment, and semantic labels for MECE comparisons, capability groups, KPI strips, matrices, and independent pillars. When numbered elements can be reordered without changing the claim, repair by removing the ordinals and strengthening grouping or relationship labels.

## Evidence And State

Classify load-bearing content as fact, estimate, assumption, anecdote, or open. Render actuals with solid emphasis and future/plan/target states with pale or dashed treatment plus labels. Use published, independently traceable sources for visible footers; keep internal inputs in the source ledger and speaker notes.

## Density

Choose a density tier before layout. Increase decision value through comparisons, benchmarks, units, denominators, assumptions, annotations, evidence strips, and supporting context. Preserve one reading path and >=20pt body text. Use the footprint targets in `core-layout-and-typography.md` so evidence occupies the canvas confidently.

## Readability and meaningful sequence

Use WCAG-backed readability targets for visible slide text: >=4.5:1 contrast for normal text and >=3:1 for large text, Japanese paragraph lines <=40 CJK glyphs, ragged-right alignment, and 1.4-1.5 line height for multi-line explanatory copy. Give each slide a unique descriptive action title. Declare one reading path before layout and verify that arrows, numbering, alignment, and grouping preserve the same sequence. Pair color with labels, shape, position, or pattern.

For quantitative comparisons, prefer common-baseline position, bars, or aligned dots. Keep area, angle, pictogram size, and decorative icons as secondary encodings. Place the decision-carrying number directly by its mark and label series directly when space permits.

## Visual Grammar

Choose the structure that makes the claim easiest to see: comparison, table, flow, roadmap, loop, matrix, KPI bridge, evidence strip, architecture stack, or restrained illustration. Use icons as sparse wayfinding. Use Honey as a selective decision signal and Petrol as the stable structural accent.
